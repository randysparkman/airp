# AI Readiness Literacy Constitution

**Version:** 0.2.0
**Status:** Working reference — authoritative source for question design, scoring rubrics, and profile generation
**Updated:** March 2026

---

## Purpose

This document defines what the AI Readiness Assessment means by "AI literacy" and establishes the authority that all downstream components reference — question generators, scoring rubrics, adaptive logic, and the profile generation prompt.

This is not a curriculum. It does not describe what people should learn. It describes the framework we use to design questions, evaluate responses, and generate readiness profiles.

---

## Foundation: The DOL AI Literacy Framework

The U.S. Department of Labor's AI Literacy Framework (February 2026) provides the foundational structure for this assessment. The DOL framework identifies five content areas that define what an AI-literate person can do. These five content areas are the territory our assessment covers.

### 1. Understand AI Principles

A foundational component of AI literacy is a clear grasp of what AI is and how it works. For workers, this does not require technical mastery, but it does require the vocabulary and mental models needed to understand how today's AI tools operate. This foundation helps demystify AI, supports more confident and accurate use, and enables workers to apply, prompt, and evaluate AI systems more effectively across a wide range of workplace scenarios.

Key content areas include: pattern recognition and probabilistic outputs (AI generates responses by identifying statistical patterns, which can result in different outputs from the same input); capabilities and modalities (text, data, image, audio); training and inference; hallucinations and accuracy limits (AI can produce confident but incorrect outputs); and human design and oversight (every AI system reflects human decisions about data, goals, and parameters).

### 2. Explore AI Uses

A core element of AI literacy is understanding how AI is being used across real-world workplace settings. Workers benefit from exposure to practical applications that illustrate how AI tools can support tasks, augment decision-making, and streamline workstreams. Because AI use varies widely by industry, occupation, and context, exploration builds familiarity and judgment — helping workers recognize when and how to apply AI effectively, and where human input remains essential.

Key content areas include: productivity tools (drafting documents, presentation outlines, analyzing reports); information support (answering questions, surfacing background information); creative assistance (generating drafts of marketing copy, naming ideas, graphic options); task-specific applications (writing code, transcribing audio, automating data entry, organizing schedules); and decision-support systems (generating recommendations, risk assessments, or forecasts).

This content area extends beyond awareness of existing applications. It includes envisioning new uses, refining how AI is currently applied, and recognizing opportunities where AI could add value but is not yet being used.

### 3. Direct AI Effectively

A core element of AI literacy is understanding how to interact with AI systems in ways that produce useful and relevant results. Because most AI tools depend heavily on the input they receive, users must learn how to provide clear instructions, include necessary context, and guide the system toward better outcomes. Directing AI effectively does not require coding skills, but it does require a mental model for how to frame prompts, share information, and iterate strategically to improve the quality of responses.

Key content areas include: contextual framing (providing background, audience, tone, or goals to shape responses); prompting techniques (structuring prompts clearly, using step-by-step instructions, specifying formats); supplying relevant input data (including supporting materials or examples to improve accuracy); iterating on outputs (treating AI interactions as an ongoing process, using follow-up prompts to refine results); and avoiding vague or misleading prompts (recognizing how clarity and word choice affect outcomes).

### 4. Evaluate AI Outputs

An essential part of AI literacy is learning how to assess the quality and usefulness of AI-generated outputs. While AI can accelerate work and surface helpful insights, the results it produces still require thoughtful review. Workers need the ability to evaluate whether an output is accurate, complete, and appropriate for the task, applying their own knowledge and judgment to determine how best to use or refine what AI has provided. This evaluation skill ensures that workers remain in control of the process and that AI is used as a support tool, not a final authority.

Key content areas include: verifying factual accuracy (cross-checking against trusted sources); assessing completeness and clarity (ensuring outputs fully address the task in usable form); spotting gaps or logical errors (identifying missing steps, flawed logic, or faulty assumptions); aligning with strategic intent (evaluating whether outputs achieve the desired goal and are fit for purpose); and applying human judgment (layering in expertise, context, and discretion when deciding how to interpret, use, or revise AI-generated content).

### 5. Use AI Responsibly

Responsible use of AI is a core component of AI literacy. As AI tools become more embedded in daily workflows, workers must understand the boundaries of appropriate use — both to safeguard information and to ensure outputs are applied ethically and effectively. This includes recognizing the limits of AI authority, protecting sensitive data, complying with workplace or legal requirements, and maintaining accountability for outcomes. Workers should be equipped with practical instincts and organizational awareness to use AI tools in ways that are secure, appropriate, and aligned with professional standards.

Key content areas include: protecting sensitive information (understanding what data should not be entered into AI tools); following workplace policies and rules (awareness of organizational guidance around AI use); avoiding misuse or harm (recognizing inappropriate uses including plagiarism, impersonation, or harm); managing context-specific risks (understanding how risk varies across tasks, audiences, and sectors); and maintaining accountability (workers remain responsible for decisions and outputs produced with AI tools).

## Through-Line: Four Human Functions AI Scaffolds

Complementary to the DOL framework is a mental model that describes *why* AI matters to humans. AI scaffolds and augments four fundamental human functions. An AI-literate person holds this mental model — they understand that AI is not a single-purpose tool but a scaffold across these four functions, each with different reliability, risk, and value. They can recognize which function AI is augmenting in any given situation.

### Understand

AI helps humans comprehend, synthesize, and make sense of information. When a person encounters a complex document, an unfamiliar domain, or a large volume of data, AI can accelerate the path to understanding — summarizing, explaining, translating jargon, surfacing patterns, and connecting disparate pieces of information into a coherent picture.

What this looks like in practice: AI summarizes a lengthy report into key takeaways. AI explains a technical concept in plain language. AI compares data across multiple sources and highlights what's changed. AI helps a worker quickly orient themselves in an unfamiliar topic area.

The human role remains essential: understanding is not the same as accepting. The person must still evaluate whether the AI's synthesis is accurate, complete, and appropriate for the decisions they need to make. AI scaffolds the process of understanding — it does not replace the judgment that understanding enables.

### Express

AI helps humans communicate, articulate, and produce. When a person needs to draft, refine, translate, or restructure their thinking into a form that others can receive — written documents, presentations, emails, visual materials, code — AI can scaffold the production process by generating first drafts, suggesting structures, adjusting tone, and handling the mechanical work of putting ideas into polished form.

What this looks like in practice: AI drafts an email the person then edits for voice and accuracy. AI restructures a disorganized set of notes into a coherent outline. AI translates a technical explanation into language appropriate for a non-technical audience. AI generates a first draft of a presentation that the person then shapes and refines.

The human role remains essential: expression is not just production — it is the act of communicating meaning, intent, and judgment. The person is responsible for what gets said, to whom, and how. AI can make the process faster and the output more polished, but the substance, accuracy, and appropriateness of what is expressed remains the person's responsibility.

### Ideate

AI helps humans generate ideas, explore possibilities, and imagine. When a person faces a problem, a decision, or an opportunity, AI can expand the space of options they consider — suggesting approaches they hadn't thought of, surfacing analogies from other domains, stress-testing assumptions, and helping move from "I don't know where to start" to "here are five directions I could go."

What this looks like in practice: AI generates a list of potential solutions to a business problem. AI suggests angles for a presentation the person hadn't considered. AI role-plays as a skeptical stakeholder to help the person anticipate objections. AI helps brainstorm names, taglines, or framings for a new initiative.

The human role remains essential: ideation is generative, but not all ideas are good. The person must evaluate which possibilities are worth pursuing, which are feasible, which are appropriate for the context, and which should be discarded. AI expands the menu — the person still orders.

### Act

AI helps humans execute, automate, and build. When a person moves from deciding to doing — completing a task, running a process, building a tool, or creating a system — AI can handle mechanical steps, manage repetitive workflows, generate functional code, and turn intentions into working outputs. This includes vibecoding (using AI to write functional software through natural language), building automations (AI-powered workflows that run with minimal human intervention), and creating AI-powered tools (applications that incorporate AI as a component).

What this looks like in practice: AI fills in a spreadsheet formula and applies it across rows. AI generates a working script that processes data files automatically. AI helps a non-developer build a small internal tool through vibecoding. A person sets up an AI-powered workflow that drafts, formats, and routes reports with minimal human intervention. AI handles the mechanical steps of a process so the person can focus on the judgment-intensive steps.

The human role remains essential: the more autonomous the AI action, the greater the need for human oversight, accountability, and design judgment. A person who builds an automation is responsible for what it produces — even when they're not watching. Act at its most powerful requires the strongest judgment about what to automate, what safeguards to include, and where human involvement must remain.

### How the Four Functions Relate to the DOL Content Areas

The four functions are not a parallel taxonomy competing with the DOL framework. They are a through-line that operates within every DOL content area:

**Understand AI Principles (DOL #1):** The four functions *are* the mental model. Understanding AI means understanding that it scaffolds these four human capabilities, each with different reliability, risk, and value.

**Explore AI Uses (DOL #2):** When someone scans their work for AI opportunities, they are asking: where could AI help me understand, express, ideate, or act more effectively? The four functions provide the generative frame for exploration.

**Direct AI Effectively (DOL #3):** When someone directs AI, they are directing it to support one of these four functions. Knowing which function they are asking AI to scaffold shapes how they prompt, what context they provide, and how they iterate. Directing AI to help you *understand* something requires different input than directing it to help you *act* on something.

**Evaluate AI Outputs (DOL #4):** When someone evaluates AI output, they are evaluating whether the AI effectively scaffolded the intended function. Did it actually help me understand, or did it just produce plausible-sounding text? Did it help me express what I meant, or did it substitute its voice for mine?

**Use AI Responsibly (DOL #5):** Responsible use looks different across the four functions. Using AI to understand carries different risks (false confidence, incomplete synthesis) than using AI to act (unsupervised automation, accountability gaps). The four functions help a person reason about *where* responsible use concerns are highest in their specific situation.

## Scoring Lens: Three Constructs

The assessment scores responses using three constructs. These are not tied 1:1 to specific questions or DOL content areas. Every response can surface signal across all three. The DOL content area determines what capability is being activated. The scoring constructs determine how well the person handles it.

### Orientation

Does this person have a functional mental model for what AI is and isn't? Do they understand where these tools are useful and where they break down?

- **Emerging:** No functional mental model. Treats AI output as either reliable or useless without distinguishing. Doesn't reason about why AI behaves the way it does.
- **Developing:** Has a rough mental model. Knows AI can be wrong, mentions checking or being careful, but reasoning is intuitive rather than principled. Can articulate some limits but not consistently.
- **Demonstrating:** Has a functional mental model that holds up under pressure. Distinguishes between generation and knowledge. Adjusts trust based on task type, stakes, and context. Can explain *why* AI might fail in a specific situation, not just *that* it might.

*Orientation is the foundation. Without it, Integration and Judgment have nothing to build on. DOL Content Area 1 (Understand AI Principles) is primarily captured here.*

### Integration

Can this person translate understanding into action? Do they describe real processes for working alongside AI, or just abstract awareness?

- **Emerging:** No described process. Uses AI as a black box — puts in a request, takes what comes back. No iteration, no context-setting, no workflow.
- **Developing:** Describes some process but it's vague or inconsistent. Mentions checking output or iterating without articulating a clear approach. May describe what they'd do in general terms without specifics.
- **Demonstrating:** Describes specific, repeatable actions. Provides context to AI, iterates on output, has a workflow for incorporating AI into their tasks. Can describe how AI fits into a broader process, not just a single interaction.

*Integration is where understanding becomes behavior. DOL Content Areas 2 (Explore AI Uses) and 3 (Direct AI Effectively) are primarily captured here.*

### Judgment

Does this person's reasoning hold up when the situation gets complicated? Do they weigh stakes, context, and tradeoffs?

- **Emerging:** No evidence of tradeoff reasoning. Applies the same approach regardless of stakes or context. Doesn't consider downstream consequences, audience, or sensitivity.
- **Developing:** Shows awareness of stakes and context but reasoning stays surface-level. Mentions that some situations are riskier without articulating why or what they'd do differently. May identify a concern without describing how they'd address it.
- **Demonstrating:** Weighs tradeoffs based on stakes, audience, and context. Adjusts approach when circumstances change. Considers downstream consequences, identifies who is affected, and takes responsibility for outcomes. Reasoning is specific to the scenario, not generic.

*Judgment is the hardest to measure and the most valuable signal. DOL Content Areas 4 (Evaluate AI Outputs) and 5 (Use AI Responsibly) are primarily captured here.*

### How the Scoring Lens Works

The three constructs are not tied 1:1 to specific questions or DOL content areas. Every response can surface signal across all three. However, each construct answers a different question about the quality of the response:

- **Orientation** asks: does this person understand what's happening?
- **Integration** asks: can this person translate understanding into action?
- **Judgment** asks: does this person's reasoning hold up when it's complicated?

A scenario targeting DOL #3 (Direct AI Effectively) is still scored for all three. A person might show strong Orientation (they know what AI needs to perform well), weak Integration (they describe the concept but not a real process), and strong Judgment (they recognize when a different approach would be needed). The DOL content area determines what capability is being activated. The scoring constructs determine how well the person handles it.

## Readiness Bands

Responses are placed on a three-band readiness spectrum. Three bands is deliberately coarse — with 15 interactions, we can confidently sort into three buckets.

### Emerging

The person treats AI as either a black box or a non-issue. They don't distinguish between fluent output and accurate output. They accept or reject AI wholesale rather than situationally. They don't describe workflows, don't weigh tradeoffs, and don't adjust their approach based on context or stakes.

### Developing

The person has a rough working model. They know AI can be wrong and say so, but their reasoning is more intuitive than principled. They can describe some process for working with AI, but it's inconsistent or vague. They show awareness of limits and stakes without a consistent framework for navigating them.

### Demonstrating

The person has a functional mental model that holds up under pressure. They distinguish between generation and knowledge. They describe specific actions, not just awareness. They weigh tradeoffs, consider stakes, and adjust their approach based on context. They reason about AI at the workflow level, not just the individual task level.

## How the Constitution Drives the Assessment

### Question Design

The DOL's five content areas define the coverage territory. Every set of assessment questions should include scenarios across multiple content areas — not just #4 (Evaluate AI Outputs), which is the current overweighted area. Specifically:

- **Tier 1 (Fixed Baseline):** Domain-agnostic scenarios covering DOL #1 and #4 primarily, with secondary signal from #3 and #5. These establish baseline Orientation.
- **Tier 2 (Contextualized):** Org-fluency-grounded scenarios that should cover all five DOL content areas. This is where #2 (Explore AI Uses) and #3 (Direct AI Effectively) must be deliberately included.
- **Tier 3 (Adaptive):** Generated based on coverage gaps from Tiers 1 and 2. The summary template should flag which DOL content areas and which human functions have not yet been activated, so the question generator can target them.

### Scoring

The three scoring constructs (Orientation, Integration, Judgment) are the scoring lens applied to every response. The rubrics that implement them are downstream of this constitution — they live in the assessment JSON files and the scoring prompts. This document defines what the constructs mean; the rubrics define what each level looks like for a specific question.

### Profile Generation

The profile generation prompt interprets the full set of scored responses through the lens of this constitution. It should reflect how the respondent performed across the DOL territory, reference the four human functions as a vocabulary for describing what AI does, and use the three scoring constructs to ground its assessments. The profile is the product — it must feel like insight, not a score report.

## Relationship to External Frameworks

**DOL AI Literacy Framework (February 2026):** Foundational. The five content areas are the base of this constitution. Our assessment operationalizes them as a measurement instrument.

**Four Human Functions (Understand, Express, Ideate, Act):** Complementary mental model. Describes the four fundamental ways AI scaffolds human capability. Lives within DOL #1 as the core mental model of an AI-literate person, and operates as a through-line across all five content areas.

**Scoring Constructs (Orientation, Integration, Judgment):** Assessment-specific. These are our scoring lens — how we read signal in responses. They are not part of the DOL framework or the four functions. They are the evaluation mechanism that turns free-text responses into structured readiness data.
